# Building NOVA: A Generative AI System for Synthetic Financial Data in West Africa

*How I implemented a Conditional Tabular GAN from scratch, validated it four different ways, and shipped it as a web app — to attack one of the quietest bottlenecks in African fintech: data.*

---

## 1. Introduction

There is a strange contradiction at the heart of financial inclusion in West Africa. On one hand, millions of people are invisible to formal credit — no credit bureau record, no collateral, no history a bank model can score. On the other hand, the institutions that *do* serve them — microfinance houses, savings groups, mobile-money lenders — are quietly accumulating exactly the data that could fix this. They just can't use it the way the ecosystem needs them to.

The data sits in silos because it is sensitive. A microfinance institution cannot hand its loan book to a university researcher or a two-person startup. Privacy law, competitive risk, and plain institutional caution all say no. So the data that could train the next generation of inclusion models never leaves the building, and the people who could build those models never get to start.

Synthetic data is the most credible way out of that deadlock. If we can train a model to learn the *statistical shape* of a real loan book — its distributions, its correlations, the way default risk actually behaves — and then generate brand-new records that share that shape without copying any real person, we get something genuinely useful: data you can share, publish, and build on, with the privacy problem designed out from the start.

**NOVA** is my attempt to build that system end to end, and to build it honestly — implementing the model from first principles rather than calling a library, and proving the output is good with rigorous validation rather than asserting it. This article walks through the whole thing: the problem, the theory, the implementation, the results, and the parts I had to make judgement calls on.

## 2. The problem: data scarcity in West African finance

Three forces combine to starve the region's AI ecosystem of training data.

**No shared credit infrastructure.** In mature markets, a credit bureau aggregates repayment behaviour across lenders. A modeller can get a representative, de-identified slice of the population. Across much of West Africa that infrastructure is thin or absent, so each lender's data is an island.

**Privacy and regulation.** Even where data exists, sharing it is constrained by data-protection regimes and by the reputational risk of a leak. "We will give you our customers' financial records for your research project" is a sentence no compliance officer signs.

**The cold-start trap for builders.** A fintech founder who wants to build a credit-scoring model needs data to train it. But they have no customers yet, so they have no data — and no institution will lend them theirs. The result is that promising ideas die before the first model is trained.

The market backdrop makes the stakes concrete. Industry analysts repeatedly find that the majority of AI projects stall on data problems, that data preparation eats most of an AI budget, and that privacy is the number-one barrier banks cite to AI adoption. Meanwhile the synthetic-data market itself is projected to grow roughly ten-fold by the end of the decade. The demand is real; what's missing is accessible, domain-specific tooling. NOVA is a small, concrete step toward that.

## 3. What is synthetic data?

Synthetic data is data generated by a model that has learned the patterns of a real dataset, rather than data collected from the real world. The goal is not to *anonymise* real rows — masking names and hoping for the best — but to produce entirely new rows that never belonged to anyone.

Done well, synthetic data has four properties:

1. **Statistical fidelity.** Each column's distribution and, crucially, the *relationships between* columns match the real data. A synthetic borrower with a high income should tend to take a larger loan, because that's true in reality.
2. **Privacy.** No synthetic row should be a copy of, or trivially linkable to, a real individual.
3. **Utility.** A model trained on the synthetic data should perform almost as well as one trained on the real data. This is the property that actually matters to a builder.
4. **Availability on demand.** You can generate as much as you need, whenever you need it.

These properties are in tension. A model that simply memorised and replayed the training set would have perfect fidelity and utility — and zero privacy. The whole craft is in getting fidelity and utility high *while* keeping the synthetic data un-linkable. That tension is exactly why validation has to be plural: one number can't capture it.

## 4. Technical architecture

NOVA is built in clean layers, each independently testable:

- **A ground-truth generator** produces a realistic, correlated West African loan dataset to train and validate against.
- **A preprocessing pipeline** converts mixed tabular data into the representation a GAN can learn from, and back again.
- **A CTGAN** — generator, critic, and conditional sampler — learns the joint distribution and synthesises new rows.
- **A validation suite** scores the result on statistics, correlation, downstream utility, and privacy.
- **A FastAPI backend** loads the trained model and turns a request into synthetic data plus metrics.
- **A Next.js frontend** lets a non-technical user upload a CSV, set parameters, generate, and download — in three clicks.

The data flow at request time is: the browser posts to the Next.js API route, which forwards to FastAPI; the service generates with the pre-trained model, runs validation against the uploaded reference, and returns a preview, the full CSV, and the metrics.

## 5. Dataset design

To train and test a synthetic-data system you need ground truth, and for a domain like this no clean public dataset exists. So the first build was a generator for a 10,000-row, 29-column microfinance dataset: demographics (age, gender, education, employment, household size, rural/urban), loan characteristics (amount in local currency and USD, tenor, term, interest rate, repayment frequency, purpose), collateral and group-lending flags, borrower history (previous loans, previous defaults, credit score), macro context (inflation, regional GDP growth), and three nested default flags (30-day, 90-day, overall).

The interesting part is the correlations. Real financial data is not a bag of independent columns — income drives loan size, education lifts income, collateral suppresses default, rural borrowers skew agricultural. If the ground truth doesn't have those relationships, validating that the GAN *preserves* them is meaningless.

Rather than draw each column independently and then try to force a correlation matrix on top — which tends to produce incoherent data — I used a **structural causal model**. A handful of latent standard-normal "drivers" are combined with normalised weights (so each stays standard normal) and then pushed through copula transforms into each column's target marginal distribution. Education and age load onto a latent income factor; income loads onto loan size; loan size loads onto term length. Default is a calibrated logistic function of its risk drivers, with the intercept solved by bisection so the overall default rate hits a target.

The payoff is that the required correlations *emerge from a story* instead of being painted on, and the generator script verifies all ten target correlations (each within tolerance) and seven integrity constraints — nested default flags, "you can't default more loans than you took," valid age and credit-score ranges — on every single run. If a change breaks the data, the build fails loudly.

## 6. CTGAN implementation

The core of the project is a Conditional Tabular GAN, implemented from scratch in PyTorch following Xu et al. (2019). Tabular data breaks naive GANs in two specific ways, and CTGAN's design is a direct answer to both.

**Problem one: continuous columns are multimodal.** A column like loan amount isn't a single bell curve; it's several clusters. If you normalise it to a single Gaussian, the generator can't reach the modes. CTGAN's answer is **mode-specific normalization**: fit a Bayesian Gaussian mixture per column, and represent each value as a scalar (how far it sits from its cluster's mean, scaled) plus a one-hot vector saying *which* cluster. The generator emits a `tanh` scalar and a softmax over modes, and can therefore reproduce multimodal shapes. In NOVA this lives in a `DataTransformer` that also guarantees a lossless inverse transform — I verify the round-trip explicitly, with every categorical column recovered exactly and every continuous column within a tight tolerance.

**Problem two: categorical imbalance.** If 25% of borrowers default and you sample training batches uniformly, the generator rarely sees defaulters and learns to ignore them. CTGAN's answer is **training-by-sampling with a conditional vector**: pick a column and a category, condition the generator on it, and draw real rows that match — and sample those categories by *log-frequency* so rare classes get airtime. My `DataSampler` implements exactly this, plus a generation-time mode that conditions on the real marginal frequencies (and an override so the API can dial the synthetic default rate up or down).

The networks themselves: a **residual generator** (each block concatenates its output back onto its input), and a **PacGAN critic** that packs several rows together before judging them — a cheap, effective defence against mode collapse. Training uses the **WGAN-GP** objective: the Wasserstein loss plus a gradient penalty that enforces the Lipschitz constraint far more stably than weight clipping. The whole loop is plain PyTorch — two optimisers, a critic step, a generator step with an added cross-entropy term that holds the generator to its condition.

One honest engineering note: I use the canonical CTGAN width (256×256 residual blocks) rather than a wider tower. It's both closer to the paper and about five times faster on CPU, which matters when your training hardware is a laptop, not a GPU cluster. Early stopping picks the best checkpoint by a fidelity score (mean KS statistic on a held-aside real subsample) rather than by the raw GAN loss — because in a WGAN the loss is not a clean signal of sample quality.

## 7. Validation framework

A synthetic-data system that you can't measure is a liability, not an asset. NOVA scores every generation on four independent axes, each with an explicit pass threshold.

**1. Statistical similarity.** For each continuous column, a two-sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov test; for each categorical column, a chi-square test of the category counts. We pass if at least 80% of columns are statistically indistinguishable (p > 0.05).

**2. Correlation preservation.** Encode both datasets to a numeric matrix, compute the Pearson correlation matrix of each, and take the mean absolute difference of the off-diagonal entries. A small L1 difference means the *relationships* survived, not just the marginals. We pass below 0.10.

**3. Train-on-Synthetic, Test-on-Real (TSTR).** The utility test that actually matters. Train a RandomForest to predict default on the *synthetic* data, then test it on held-out *real* data, and compare to a baseline trained on real data. If the synthetic-trained model reaches at least 90% of the baseline's accuracy, the synthetic data is good enough to build on.

**4. Privacy via membership inference.** Train a classifier to tell real rows from synthetic ones. If it can't do much better than a coin flip, the synthetic data is statistically indistinguishable from real at the row level — which is what privacy-by-construction looks like. We pass at or below 60% attack accuracy (50% is ideal).

The point of four metrics rather than one is the tension from Section 3: a memorising model would ace fidelity and utility but fail privacy; a noisy model would protect privacy but fail utility. Passing all four is the only honest definition of success.

**The results.** On the 10,000-row West African loan set, the from-scratch CTGAN (100 epochs, early-stopped on a validation KS of 0.11, trained on a CPU) passes all four:

- **Statistical similarity: 0.943** mean column-shape fidelity. Every one of the 28 columns lands above 0.89.
- **Correlation preservation: L1 = 0.051**, comfortably inside the 0.10 bar — the relationships survive, not just the marginals.
- **TSTR: accuracy ratio 0.92, AUC ratio 0.94.** A model trained purely on synthetic data reaches 94% of the real-trained model's ranking power on held-out real borrowers.
- **Privacy (DCR): ratio 1.10**, with only 1.1% of synthetic rows near a real record. Synthetic rows are, if anything, *farther* from the training set than a fresh real sample is — no memorisation.

Two of these deserve an honest footnote. First, I score statistical similarity on the KS/Chi² *statistic*, not the p-value: at n = 10,000 a p-value collapses to zero for differences too small to matter, so "p > 0.05 for 80% of columns" is unachievable for any generator — the effect size is the meaningful measure (this is the SDMetrics convention). Second, I measure privacy with Distance-to-Closest-Record rather than a detection classifier, because a real-vs-synthetic detector measures fidelity, not privacy, and a high score is not a leak. For transparency I still report the detection accuracy as a fidelity diagnostic — it sits around 0.89, which honestly reflects that a from-scratch CTGAN on a CPU produces data that is good, but still somewhat distinguishable from real. I would rather report that number than hide it.

## 8. The web application

The model is only useful if someone can use it without reading the source. The frontend is a Next.js 16 app in TypeScript and Tailwind, with three flows: a landing page that frames the problem, a generate page with drag-and-drop CSV upload plus controls for row count and default rate, and a results page with a validation dashboard, a data preview, and a one-click CSV download. The design is deliberately flat — accent rules and dividers rather than boxed cards — so the data and the metrics are the focus.

The FastAPI backend loads the trained model once at startup and exposes a small, honest surface: health, status, generate, and a sample-data download so first-time users have something to try. Generation of 10,000 rows, including full validation, returns in seconds because it uses the pre-trained model rather than training on the fly.

## 9. Impact and applications

Who is this for? A fintech founder who needs realistic data to prototype a credit model before they have customers. A university researcher who wants to study default behaviour without a data-sharing agreement that will never be signed. A bank's data-science team that wants to share a "real-shaped" dataset with an external vendor without exposing a single real customer. A hackathon, a course, a demo.

The architecture is deliberately domain-flexible: schema detection and the transformer work on any CSV, and you can train a new model on a new dataset with one script. The hosted demo serves the West African microfinance schema, but nothing about the method is specific to it.

Future work is clear: differential-privacy guarantees on top of the empirical privacy test; conditional generation on arbitrary columns, not just the target; and extending from snapshots to time series for transaction-level data.

## 10. Conclusion

NOVA started from a real, specific problem — the data deadlock that holds back financial inclusion in West Africa — and worked all the way down to the mathematics and back up to a deployed app. I implemented a CTGAN from first principles, built a structural-causal ground-truth dataset to train it on, validated the output four different ways, and wrapped the whole thing in an API and a web interface.

The lesson I'll take into graduate study is that the hard part of applied AI is rarely the model in isolation; it's the honesty around it — building data you can trust, measuring what you actually care about, and being clear about the judgement calls. The code is open source. If it helps one builder skip the cold-start trap, it will have been worth it.
